Overview

What is
this project?

Fighter AI is an intelligent fighting game agent that plays against a human opponent in real time. Two neural networks operate in a chained pipeline every frame: a bidirectional LSTM reads the human's move history and predicts their next action, then a 1D CNN uses that prediction alongside the current game state to select the optimal counter-action.

Both models were first trained with supervised learning on 1 million+ frames of scripted bot self-play, then fine-tuned using reinforcement learning — two CNN agents playing against each other until both converge on strategic fighting behaviour beyond what any scripted bot could teach.

A physical Arduino-controlled servo rotates 5 times whenever the AI fighter is defeated, providing a tangible physical robot response to the simulation outcome.

Evaluation

Results & Metrics

LSTM Confusion Matrix

Per-class move prediction accuracy across all 8 action classes. Final test accuracy 46.6%, macro F1 0.373 over 30 epochs. Class-weighted loss prevented collapse onto the dominant IDLE class. Macro F1 reported to penalise poor performance on rare moves (Block, Uppercut) equally to common ones (Jump, Idle).

LSTM Per-Class ROC Curves

One-vs-rest ROC curves for each of the 8 move classes. AUC computed per class. Classes with AUC > 0.85 indicate the LSTM reliably distinguishes that move from all others.

CNN Counter-Action Confusion Matrix

Counter-action classification accuracy across 10 AI actions including the two walk actions added for movement. Supervised test accuracy 21.7%, F1 0.179 — reflecting the inherent difficulty of imitating strategic counter-actions from bot data. RL self-play was essential to convert this into a competitively effective agent.

RL Self-Play Win Rate

Agent 1 reached a 72.2% win rate over 2000 reinforcement learning episodes against Agent 2 (23.2% wins, 4.7% draws). Fights began decisively from episode 40 as agents learned to close distance and deal damage, with average fight length dropping from 3600 frames (full timeout) to under 1500 frames by episode 100.

Ablation Study — CNN alone vs CNN + LSTM

100 rounds per condition. CNN+LSTM pipeline consistently outperforms CNN alone, validating the integration of both models.

Reflection

Results Discussion
& Future Work

✅

What worked well

Chaining LSTM and CNN into a single inference pipeline produced an AI that demonstrably adapts to player patterns. The ablation study confirmed the LSTM input meaningfully improves CNN counter-action selection — 95% vs 92% against the aggressive bot. RL self-play drove agent 1 to a 72.2% win rate over 2000 episodes with only 4.7% draws, far beyond what supervised training alone achieved. Inference runs at under 1ms per frame on GPU, well within the 16ms real-time budget. Class-weighted cross-entropy successfully prevented the LSTM from ignoring rare moves. The temperature parameter gave fine-grained difficulty control with no additional training.

⚠️

Limitations

The CNN supervised F1 of 0.18 (10-class) reflects the difficulty of learning counter-action strategy from imitation alone — RL self-play was essential to make the AI competent. The random agent produced 0% win rate in ablation because it rarely closes distance, meaning fights never started — this is a limitation of the opponent design rather than the AI. The LSTM window of 10 frames may be too short to detect slow-developing combo patterns, and the custom Pygame environment lacks the visual richness of a commercial fighting game which limits demo impact.

🔭

Future work

Replacing the structured feature vector with raw pixel input would allow a true CNN vision pipeline processing game frames directly. Extending RL training with PPO instead of vanilla reinforcement learning would improve sample efficiency and stability — the reinforcement learning's high variance was visible in the noisy win-rate curve. Integrating a Transformer-based move predictor as an ablation against the LSTM would complete the model comparison story. Increasing training matches beyond 300 and RL episodes beyond 2000 would likely push win rates higher.
